Found

by Daniela Maestres

The horse’s skull lay in a bend on the dirt path. It had a clean and even set of teeth. Before I thought about it, I reached to pluck one of them out. It took me a second to choose the right one. It jiggled in place, but was firmly attached. After some pulling, tugging, and swearing, it came loose. The sun had warmed the tooth and it had an unfamiliar shape and texture. I placed it in the pocket of my camera bag and it’s been there since. I tend to forget about it, but every now and then I reach in to look for a coin and meet its sharp edges. It takes me a second to recognize what it is; then I remember the horse from Magdalena. It’s a friendly reminder that we should show our teeth as much as possible. Who knows how much longer we are gonna have them.

Song

by Dave Eggers

The man is in a hotel just outside Nairobi. A local friend of his has set him up here, saying the hotel was clean and cheap.

It is indeed cheap, forty-five dollars a night, but it does not seem safe. The locks on the doors do not work, and the clerk sits behind bulletproof glass. The man’s room is small and smells of paint, and the outlets don’t work, so he goes down to the hotel bar, which is unadorned and empty.

He orders a gin and tonic from the Kenyan bartender, who is wearing a red vest and white shirt and black bowtie. The visitor drinks his first drink down, feeling sad about so many things, wanting to be home, wanting to be doing something else, and then orders another. He takes this second drink and, not wanting the bartender to feel compelled to talk to him, he walks to the other side of the bar and sits in front of a large flat-screen television. There is a music video on, in which eight African women in traditional clothing are singing and playing instruments. The song is jangly, buoyant, joyous.

The man goes back to the bartender to ask who the band is, this all-woman band in the music video. The bartender says he has no idea, so the visitor goes back to his seat and finishes watching the music video. When it’s done, he sees the name of the band on the bottom left corner, writes it down, and finishes his drink. He looks up to find the bartender at his side.

Everywhere in the world, men who are alone or who seem alone get off airplanes or leave their hotels, and they get in taxis, and they ask the drivers of these taxis to take them to bars, or nightclubs, or to hear live music. And these taxi drivers, without heed and almost without exception, take these men to brothels.

Once, in Bangkok, accompanying a few friends, one much older Russian and one younger American, both of whom thought the whole enterprise—the brothels, Thailand, life altogether—absurd, the visitor found himself in a nightclub where young Thai women danced on steel stages and whose attentions could be rented by the hour. A slightly older Thai woman, holding a clipboard, urged the visitor to spend some time with a young woman in the back room, and the Russian said, “Do it, do it,” and laughed maniacally.

She quickly disrobed, revealing a body that looked much like the man’s own, at age twelve.

Because he was a young man seeking any new experience, the visitor took his notebook and he went with the young Thai woman, feeling wretched as he followed her away from the lights of the club and through a back passageway. The young woman was tiny, shy of a hundred pounds, and led him through a series of narrow hallways, everything lit indigo, and into a white room. She closed the door. On one half of the room there was a shower, white-tiled and so big a dozen people could wash themselves at once. On the other side of the room there was a kind of bed, high like one found in a medical examination room, with a plastic white mattress.

The young woman motioned him to sit, and he did. She quickly disrobed, revealing a body that looked much like the man’s own, at age twelve. With a stern downward motion, she suggested the man take his clothes off too, but he disabused her of any obligations to work his flesh. He told her he would still pay her for her time, but he wanted only to sit and rest and talk. She understood immediately; it was not an unusual turn of events, it seemed, for a visitor to want no part of the whole thing, to instead want to buy an hour of respite for them both. She scooted back and rested against the wall.

He asked her name, and she said it was Song.

“Song?” he asked.

She nodded, then took his pen and wrote the word in his notebook, in careful, impeccable English. “SONE.”

Oh, Sone. The visitor assumed some clever English-speaking madam, maybe the woman with the clipboard, had devised this name, knowing it would be intriguing, sounding like something between a song and a moan, something between music and despair.

The visitor nodded, smiled, told her she wrote well in English, and found there was nothing more to say. He drew her a few pictures in his notebook, and then, the two of them frustrated by their inability to say anything to each other, lay side by side for the remainder of their time, the music thumping through the walls, the room otherwise as still as a grave. He briefly fell asleep.

In the bar of the Kenyan hotel, the man smiles and tells the bartender no, he needs no company tonight. He goes up to his room and tries to get online, where he looks up the all-woman band he saw on the television, hoping to buy the music they made.

Dave Eggers is the author of seven books, most recently A Hologram for the King. Eggers is the founder and editor of McSweeney’s, an independent publishing house based in San Francisco, cofounder of 826 Valencia, a nonprofit writing and tutoring center for youth, and the founder of ScholarMatch, an organization that connects under-resourced students with resources, schools and donors to make college possible.

Jambú

by Eleanor Stanford

541

Some such Spice--express and pass--
Subject to Your Plucking--

Emily Dickinson

The jambú looks like a cross between a pear and an apple, but the taste most closely approximates waterlogged Styrofoam. There was a jambú tree in our condominium, which dropped its fruit on the cobblestone path near the swimming pool. 

Even the marmosets that lived in the trees turned up their noses, spoiled by the cookies and bits of ripe banana that the children fed to them.


In a tropical climate, many fruits are available year round: papayas, bananas, jackfruit. The plants are hardy, amenable, long-suffering. They give and give and give. Modern commerce makes up for much of the rest; as in most places in the world now, you can go to the supermarket and buy apples shipped in from Chile and kiwis from New Zealand. 

But many other fruits are rare and seasonal, impossible to find except for during a small window of the year. They aren’t cultivated anywhere else, at least not on a scale that would warrant import and export. Most wouldn’t transport, anyway. Many are harvested from wild stands and, being undomesticated, are shy, temperamental, difficult to control. Indoors, they spoil quickly, or lose their vibrancy, turn sullen in a bowl on the counter, refuse to ripen.


The jambú, or water apple, one source declares, is consumed mainly by children. Perhaps adults have lost the taste for them, spoiled by expectations of what a fruit should taste like. Or we’ve lost the elemental pleasure in picking a fruit from the tree or the ground and eating it, a satisfaction whole and complete in itself, one that eclipses the actual taste of the fruit. 

Our nanny, Dete, showed my children how to eat jambús. When they ripened and began to fall she took them to the tree, showed them how to find a good one—not too hard, not too soft. To check for rot, to rub it on the hem of their shirts before biting into it.

She alighted, breezy and pragmatic, on the low wall of the veranda, and took the baby on her lap.

She came to work for us two days after we arrived in Brazil. She alighted, breezy and pragmatic, on the low wall of the veranda, and took the baby on her lap. Papai, she called him, a term of endearment which literally means, Daddy.

Before I met Dete, I had not been able to imagine leaving my three-month-old with anyone. Yet, seeing him in her arms, the ease with which he went to her, made me begin to question that. 

It was as though the self I had constructed so carefully, the strands of my identity I had wrapped around myself (I would never leave my child with a nanny; mothers who do that are irresponsible, or in denial; why have children just to leave them in someone else’s care?), began to unravel.

I remembered the story of Nabokov, lover of butterflies, who, as a boy, kept a hawkmoth pupa unhatched for seven years. Until he took it on a train, and as they pulled away from the station in St. Petersburg, its furred body unfolded and it fluttered through the cabin. 

Was it the voyage that caused the transformation, the motion of the train, the act of travel? Or had its time simply arrived, at last? 

It had been almost that long, I realized, that I had been home with my children, since I had imagined a life outside that tight cocoon.

I remembered the story of Nabokov, lover of butterflies, who, as a boy, kept a hawkmoth pupa unhatched for seven years.

At work, there were good days and bad days those first few weeks. There were students who sought me out for help with college applications, who were eager to go over their school lists and essays with me. There were eighth graders who sat in my office stony-faced and unresponsive. There were hours I sat at my desk wondering what I was supposed to be doing.

But it didn’t even matter so much what I was doing. I liked having somewhere to go and something to do. I liked slipping into a dress Dete had ironed for me, putting on the dangly amethyst earrings I wouldn’t be able to wear at home because the baby would have tugged on them.

From the very beginning, I imagined taking Dete back to the States with us or, on my better days, living in Brazil indefinitely, the two of us growing old together. 

I began to understand why my husband’s parents had brought their nanny with them when they emigrated. Of course, that was a different era, when visas were easier to come by. And they were both doctors, who could afford to support a nanny in the U.S. But I saw how a nanny quickly becomes part of the family. She is not easily replaceable; to keep a good one, I could already see, one would do almost anything. 

In my more sober, realistic moments, I realized that my relationship with Dete was fleeting, lapidary, destined to disappear. There was no way it could be transplanted to the cold Philadelphia winter. 

I knew that, sooner or later, as Dickinson says, the License closes. 

Even though Dete said plainly from the beginning that she’d want to go, for her to do so would have taken enormous effort, subject to the whims of immigration laws, figuring out how to bring her own young children with her, finding a job for her in the States (or enough money to pay her ourselves). 

I thought of our passports in a drawer upstairs, their wings folded—the blue U.S. ones, the viridescent Brazilian ones.

I thought of the butterfly exhibit at the Academy of Natural Sciences in Philadelphia that my sons used to love: the blue morpho and paper kite, swallowtails and firetips in their steamy indoor garden, flying, resting on the fragile tropical plants, pupating behind the steamy glass.

As it was, the inherent imbalances between Dete and me—class, education, age, experience—were compensated by the fact that I was the foreigner: my halting Portuguese; her fluency in the language of food and the natural world; my loneliness, adrift in an unfamiliar country; her familiarity with everyone in the condominium—everyone, it seemed, in the city of Salvador.

Dete was no more subject to my plucking than the jambús, staring down from their high branches; no more subject to my plucking than the unfamiliar constellations my husband and I stared at in the evenings, swaying in the hammock on our veranda. 

In the morning I would be a foreigner, too, again. I would rise in frustration and in awe at the gentle, early light, the jambú tree outside spreading its blossoms like small bright-pink asterisks across the ground.

What did it mean to be a foreigner? I had chosen this, and in truth there were many aspects of it that I enjoyed. It gave a name to the feeling of exile. It justified the oddness, the slight out-of-synchness I have felt—that surely everyone feels, one time or another—everywhere. It gave a thrilling, manic edge to the most quotidian routines. It made me see things with new eyes: grocery stores and traffic jams, trees and flowers.

The jambú tree flowers on both the trunk and the branches, florescent pink puffballs that disperse in the breeze like confetti. 

A German naturalist traveling in Brazil in the 1930s wrote, “[It] was always a pleasure, when I left the monastery garden for the street, to pass through an alley of Jambo-trees, which, with their crimson flowers, set like candles on the branches and glowing in the green foliage, looked as though they had been illuminated.”

We had only one tree, but I can imagine a row of them, and how they might provide a sort of luminescent passageway from one space to another, from the cloistered abbey to the street.

Little cultural attention has been given the water apple. Where are the odes to the water apple, the songs and paeans, the biography of its thick petioles, its crooked trunk and open crown?

Where are the jambú orchards, the fields of dull, light-green foliage, the fruit thin-skinned and shining on the branches?

Also known in English as water apple, or watery rose apple, the appeal of the jambú is largely its thirst-relieving character. It is a blank, a placeholder. Small aqueous globe onto which you draw your own map: continent, coastline, tortuous passage.

We were nowhere near the pampas (which are in Argentina and southern Brazil, thousands of miles from Salvador), but does it matter? Dickinson’s butterfly—like Dickinson’s Brazil—is a mythical creature, an exquisite apparition.

Dickinson is an exact geographer, but she’s no tour guide to South America. Her maps of dark places are ethereal, made of light and feathers, gossamer and tulle.

For a more concrete cartography, I turned not to the Rough Guide or Lonely Planet, which always seemed useless, out of date, too obvious or too obscure, but to the early explorers.

The jambú is a blank, a placeholder. Small aqueous globe onto which you draw your own map: continent, coastline, tortuous passage.

Though perhaps there’s not much difference between Cabral or Darwin on the storm-tossed Atlantic and Dickinson in her Amherst attic, embarking on the great scow of her imagination.

Or Alexander von Humboldt, who, on a voyage to this same continent in 1799, wrote:

A strange, completely unknown feeling is awoken in us when nearing the equator and crossing from one hemisphere to another… The stars we have known since infancy begin to vanish.

“And new ones appear, blinking on slowly, in the gentle equinoctial dark.”

In my small Brazil, I gathered the wild fruits that Dete named for me: jambú, sapotí, pitanga. They provided a map of sorts, too—a chart of seasons and the subtle shifting of the light; of trees and bushes; of childhood, that undomesticated realm of hiding places and negotiations.

The jambú is almost always eaten out of hand—an impulsive find, sweet afterthought, windfall. But if you take a knife to it, press a deliberate blade against its skin on the cutting board, a cross-section of the fruit reveals a luminous cavity spoked with seeds, a hollow star.

Eleanor Stanford is the author of História, História: Two Years in the Cape Verde Islands (Chicago Center for Literature and Photography) and The Book of Sleep (Carnegie Mellon Press). Her poems and essays have also appeared in Poetry, The Harvard Review, The Iowa Review, and others. She lives in the Philadelphia area.

Maine Camp

by Perry Benson

Sunday, July 30, 1978

Up pretty early. Beeze, Cro, and I went down to the landing to get a rotten dock together. We replaced a few boards and then put an outboard on one end of it, and Beeze and I took it up to the upper camp with the boys. An approaching thunderstorm didn’t seem to faze those at the camp, but we saw very vivid lightning as we proceeded along at a quarter knot per hour, if that. Honor and Alix walked the gravity-feed waterline and ran into problems. Cro took over, essentially connecting old galvanized pipes with tightly wound strips of rubber from inner tubes. I had brought some up for swimming, but they served a better purpose along the waterline. We have water! It was impossible working on the line, as the bugs were terrible and there was a lot less plastic (PVC) piping. Sid Fox brought more plastic last year after Honor was here. It rained on and off all day and we swam that afternoon after an amazing rainstorm. Cro and his kids left for Southwest Harbor…turkey for dinner for us.

Monday, July 31

Beeze and I put the two Sailfish in the water. It was quite hot and I capsized many times. Anna and Andrew Ingersoll seem to be quite content. I worked on the porch. Honor and Charlie went into town and came back to find the docks in place. Honor’s hamburger casserole for dinner, then we played Password.

Tuesday, August 1, 1978

We all went to the landing at Four Corners: Charlie, Beeze, and I to get wood and Honor and Tish to find raspberries. After enough wood, I went to join the raspberry-picking crowd. We walked up an unknown road, ending up at the far end of Sugar Berth then bushwhacking along its shore. Lots of scrapes on the ladies’ legs. We found the canoe at the water-pipe inlet and walked down along the stream to the lower camp and the final leg to the upper cabins. Charlie and Alix had discovered that the propane tank was empty. Honor and I went to town in search of gas. No one was at the tanks, so we went to find Gary Hall, the brother of the gas distributor. We never saw Gary but we saw a truck full of propane tanks on 201 and made a U-turn, following it to the distributor for a replacement tank. Sailing for me in the p.m. followed by ham for dinner brought by the Ingersolls and poker. Beeze was the big winner and I was the big loser. A late evening.

Edward arranged two long bamboo poles, and at the top of the makeshift mast he’d hung a large Union Jack.

Wednesday, August 2, 1978

Honor, Alix, the kids, and I went down to the dam to fish. Not one fish, but plenty of insect bites. After lunch at the camp I sailed up to the mouth of the Dead Waters and saw two loons and their baby very close up, twenty-five feet away. Incredible noises. Later Honor and I were going to fish up in the Dead Waters, but threatening skies prohibited it. A storm never materialized. Charlie’s Swiss cheese fondue for dinner. Very strong.

A lazy morning until lunch. Alix went for a swim, though she is a bit put off by the unknowns in the bottom of the lake. Yesterday, the kids, Alix, Tish, and I went up to Picnic Rock up the lake to pick blueberries for a pie. It was very buggy up there, but there was an extraordinary amount of blueberries. So Tish made a blueberry pie today. Yesterday she made brownies, which were immediately devoured by the kids and me.

Charlie, the kids, and I went across the lake to look for the trail to the top of Burnt Jacket Mountain. We found lots of bugs and pretty mushrooms. In the afternoon, I found that I was running low on smokes, so I was elected to go into town to get groceries and wine. On the way home in the boat I just missed getting soaked by an incredible, fast-moving thunderstorm. I landed just as the lightning and rain came, but I got the food through! The strong winds pushed the swimming dock down the lake. Leftovers for dinner.

Thursday, August 3

Up very early, at 6:30, and all eight of us piled into the boat and went up to the Dead Waters to check out the wildlife. We were up there for a long time and saw nothing, but many mosquitoes saw us and even though we were covered with Cutter it didn’t work for the hungriest of the mosquitoes. We went around a bend and saw a huge bull moose, his backside to us, but he was there. He stayed in our view a long time, it seemed, until annoyed at something he proceeded into the thicket of alder bushes, woods. We continued up the meandering stream at the NW end of the lake, seeing Cedar Waxwings and a Kingfisher. We turned around and headed home for breakfast.

Friday, August 4, 1978

The rains kept up with an incredible wind. Then the clouds moved on, but the wind continued. Beeze and Charlie went out on one Sailfish and I went out on the other. The sailing was just incredible. Jim Hamilton arrived around five on a seaplane. A class act. He arrived with the normal amount of luggage and fishing equipment beyond belief: two rods and a steamer-trunk-sized tackle box. The first thing he did after getting settled was to go off to the end of the dock and give it a try. He caught four “chub,” but since they are junk fish he squeezed them to crush their air sacs. Later in the afternoon we saw an osprey carry off one of the dead fish. Dinner of chicken and rice and a cake for Honor as we celebrated her birthday with champagne brought in by Jim. Pretty chilly tonight, so all in by fire in living room.


Saturday, August 5

Not much of a wind, broken high clouds and sun threatened by a dropping thermometer. Alix and Beeze left around eleven. Honor and Jim took them down as Charlie and I went to retrieve the swimming dock. Strange yells came from across the lake. Charlie yodeled a bit to egg them on, but they stopped as quickly as they started.

Tish has been painting oils on prepared paper with nice quality of color. I found an easel in my new room, having given up my first to Jim. Honor has been keeping herself busy trying to neaten up the place. We were the first group here this year. Mouse and bat shit is the messiest stuff. Rotten boards on the decks seem to be a major problem or will be in time.

Jim, Honor, and I went off in a motorboat to fish. Nothing, of course, but we did see an extraordinary sight at the other end of the lake which we couldn’t figure out. Honor pulled out a pair of binoculars and saw that it was Meg and Edward Copsey rowing to camp in a dinghy that Edward pulled out of lower camp’s kitchen. Edward arranged two long bamboo poles and at the top of the makeshift mast he’d hung a large Union Jack. It was an amazing sight and reminded me of the German ship on the lake in The African Queen. Their colorful arrival was met by a reception flotilla of Honor, Jim, and I in an outboard and Charlie and Tish with Anna and Andrew in a canoe. Edward immediately took charge in the kitchen and we switched from half-gallon to gallon bottles of wine. It was quite chilly and damp with occasional sprinkles outside as we sat by a fire inside after dinner…

Perry Benson is an architect and artist in Philadelphia.

Three Trips

by Lauren Doty and Juan Leguizamon

While the best souvenirs from trips are memories, we document our travels with pictures, videos or journals. Every time we travel overseas we immerse ourselves in the culture and explore the country. Our goal is to capture the essence of the place and share it in a narrative way. Rather than visit and pose next to typical attractions, we find our own hidden treasures and highlight them in different ways. We’re after urban legends, local eateries, secret trails and hidden gems. We play with our cameras and go on filmmaking missions, like shooting bicycles riding past timeless architecture or filming each other’s backs while strolling through town or exploring a trail. The technique enhances the experience for us, especially when locals laugh and think we’re crazy.

Juan is originally from Colombia and has spent the past 10 years living and working in San Francisco as an Art Director. Lauren has spent most of her life in California working as an Accounting Manager for various companies in the music or gaming industries. A new work opportunity for Juan transported them both to Brooklyn in 2013. While Juan works in the creative world and Lauren in business, they complement each other by exploring the outdoors and scheming grand adventures. This adventurous spirit emerged on their second date when they went on a hike ill-prepared in Point Reyes, California. Sundown came earlier than expected and they found themselves lost in the woods. They spent the night wrapped in a picnic blanket continuously clicking two empty beer bottles, hoping it would deter mountain lions in the brush. This was only the beginning of many adventures to come.

Driving with Poppa

by Alec Wilkinson

During the first week of August we met in Los Angeles, to drive to Tucson. Neutrino had taken the tape off the side of the van. Bits of dust and sand had clung to the edges of the adhesive left behind, so you could still read “Key West to Cuba,” faintly, as if the trip had already been accomplished. 

So that I would be comfortable, Neutrino vacuumed his car. Because he couldn’t get all the dog hair off the passenger seat, he covered it with a coat. Like a Frenchwoman from the historical past, Neutrino often applies perfume instead of bathing. He uses the cheapest fragrances, typically ones he buys at a drugstore. We would be crossing the Mojave Desert. Neutrino thought that he could cool the car for us by filling the interior with ice. He bought four plastic laundry baskets and put them in the back, beside his bed. Before he picked me up he filled the baskets with bags of ice.

Like a Frenchwoman from the historical past, Neutrino often applies perfume instead of bathing.

We left Santa Monica after breakfast. By the time we reached Palm Springs, the day was hot. The sky was a pale blue. Neutrino said that what he had loved about the Bedouin’s life he had lived in the desert was that you wake up and start to move. On the dashboard was a paperback copy of the Bhagavad-Gita. The dog sat between us, on a blanket. Now and then, Neutrino would pour water on the dog. The water would splash on me, but I don’t think he knew it, because he was watching the road. Then he would pat the dog and more water would fly off her. Then the dog would shake. Neutrino fell in behind the slowest-moving truck on the road. He figured that any vehicle coming toward us that crossed into our lane would hit the truck. Cars passed us going so fast that it was as if they were fleeing something. Every few minutes, Neutrino would stick one hand through the curtain between the seats. “Yeah, that’s working,” he’d say. “I can really feel it cooler now. You feel it?” The water leaked onto the floor and made the red dye on the side of my canvas in-flight bag run over everything inside the bag and stain it red, but I didn’t know that until later. About once an hour we would stop and empty the water from the plastic bags, and every two hours we would buy more ice. 

In the desert there were so many high-tension wires that they divided the sky like lines on sheet-music paper. We crossed bridges over dry rivers. The riverbeds and the land around them were the color of linen and looked more like beaches than riverbeds. A sign said one of them was the Gila River.

Near Phoenix, we left the highway and drove through fields and past warehouses and stores until we came to a dusty lot and a building that was low and long, with a roof hanging over the front of it, shading the door. A handsome, dark-haired Mexican woman was sweeping a porch in front of the door, and when we turned into the parking lot, she leaned her broom against the doorframe and stepped inside. A man with black hair and a face the color of tobacco met us. Neutrino said, “Remember me?” The man smiled and said hello. “I just wanted to show my friend the raft,” Neutrino said. “That all right?” We followed the man through the door, into a small, sleepy store that sold toys and children’s clothes and CD’s of Mexican singers and drinks, then through another door into a garage that had tires and tools spread around it, then into another dusty lot where there were a few trucks and beat-up and broken-down cars and, on a trailer, like a rare bird among a flock of sparrows, Neutrino’s raft. It was the first time I had seen it.

When I recall the moment, I feel my eyes widen. I expected a sleek, handmade craft, a stylish vessel, varnished and elegant, a craft to bear up to the life-and-death task being asked of it, and it was a scrappy, scuffed-up, bummy looking, broken-down wreck of doghouse on a floor of plywood. It looked like something a child had made. It had a flat bed, and there was a cabin about four feet tall at the back of it, and in front of the cabin a deck large enough for Neutrino to sit in a folding chair. No joint was tight. Where there were gaps, you could see pieces of drink coolers. Some of the plywood was new and some was splintered and peeling and had weathered to a dark gray. The back of the cabin was cut from a sign that Neutrino had painted in Key West for Katha Sheehan, a woman he met who was running for mayor. Sheehan and her husband had given him his van and his dog. Dark yellow letters on a yellow ground spelled “atha for ayor.” Screws were driven into the boards at angles, and the intervals between them were uneven.

“How long did it take to build?” I asked.

“About four hours,” he said. 

I nodded. 

“I designed her for speed,” he said. 

I wrote “Speed” in my notebook, then I underlined it, because I couldn’t think of anything to say. 

“She’ll ride high in the water, just plane,” he said. He extended his hand slowly, with the palm toward the ground. 

“The holes aren’t a problem?” 

“Nope.”

Neutrino pointed to the place on the side of the cabin where he meant to build outriggers. Then we walked back through the garage and bought some bottled water from the woman at the store. Neutrino told the man he would send money for the next month’s rent. We got back in the van and drove toward Phoenix, and when we saw a motel from the highway I said, “That looks OK,” and we pulled off and I got a room. We ate dinner in a Mexican restaurant where there were a lot of families and a man who went around the room playing guitar and singing. Neutrino gave him five dollars and spoke to him in Spanish, and they settled on a song for him to sing.

Before I left Neutrino that night he wrote my name and room number on a sheet of paper and put it on the van’s dashboard, in case the motel checked the cars in its lot, then he walked the dog and went to sleep in the back of the van. 

The next morning the road laid out in front of us like a carpet the color of tin.

Alec Wilkinson has been a writer at The New Yorker since 1980.  Before that he was a rock and roll musician and before that he was a policeman in Wellfleet, Massachusetts, on Cape Cod.  He has published ten books, including Big Sugar, A Violent Act, The Happiest Man in the World, and The Ice Balloon.

Notebook

These notes were written in Calexico, over dinner, as Neutrino was contemplating leaving for Asia from Baja on his raft. On the left hand side of the page, he is saying, “I have money, I have papers, I have seclusion, what will stop me?  I have to be careful nobody steals Boop, or the engine.”  Boop was his dog, a female Boston terrier. On the right hand page he is expressing a core Neutrino belief. “I don’t want an OK life. I’m not even sure what it is. I’m into Wow, and if I can’t have it, I don’t even want to be around for an OK life.” Beneath the line it says, “I don’t want to see what happens when I get on this raft...” Neutrino hated surprises. He tried to plan so carefully as to eliminate them.—Alec Wilkinson
